Letters of recomendation

What students need to know:

Letters of recommendation are a critical component of many academic and professional applications, such as college admissions, scholarship applications, job applications, and graduate school admissions.

Choose recommenders who know you well, and approach them well in advance. Personalizing the request and providing necessary information, such as resumes and program details, is essential. Following application guidelines, being courteous, and expressing gratitude are key considerations. When asking the rule of thumb is the sooner, the better.

Students might consider waiving their rights to view the letters for added credibility. Keeping track of requests and maintaining post-application relationships with recommenders can prove valuable. Remember, while recommendation letters play a significant role, they are just one component of a comprehensive application package; ensure that all aspects of your application reflect your qualifications and aspirations.

What recommenders need to know:

Ask the applicant for a copy of their resume, cover letter, personal essay, or any other documents they’re submitting as part of their application. Read through them thoroughly and avoid repeating any of the information mentioned unless it’s particularly important.

Do some research on the university or scholarship the applicant is targeting, and customize your letter based on their requirements.

Before writing your letter, think about the tone you should use and the details you want to highlight. This gives your letter of recommendation the best chance of leaving a positive first impression.

The heading that appears at the top of your recommendation letter should include your name and contact details. Not only does it look more professional, but it conveniently provides the employer or institution with a way to reach out to you, should they need more information.

Your first body paragraph should start by mentioning 2–3 of the applicant’s specific skills, talents, or experiences that are relevant to their college program and qualities of their application.Finish your body with a sentence or two highlighting some of the applicant’s key strengths or personality traits. Your letter closing should be formal and polite.

School profile

What is a school profile?

A school profile is a concise snapshot of a secondary school. It’s not always required, but a school profile can be a powerful component of a student’s college or university application. School profiles are used increasingly in other countries to add context to student applications and their schools background.

Each school is unique and there is no single template for a perfect school profile, but this may help!

A school profile is a critical document for college admissions and should include school contact details, demographics like class size, curriculum options, GPA and class rank calculations if relevant, and post-graduation statistics. Optionally, it can contain standardized test performance, community overviews, extracurricular activities, and university enrollment lists for recent graduating cohorts. However, it does not need mission statements, facility pictures, student awards, or profiles of staff. It should be concise, well-organized with clear headers, and use data when available for easy readability by admissions offices.

Transcripts

What students need to know:

Transcripts are essential academic records that every student should understand. They serve as official documentation of a student's academic history, containing crucial information like course titles, grades, credit hours, and cumulative GPA. It's important to note that there are two types of transcripts: official and unofficial. Official transcripts are typically required for formal applications, such as college admissions or job applications, and they are usually sent directly from the educational institution to the recipient. Unofficial transcripts, on the other hand, are often for personal reference and can be accessed through the school's student portals.

Students must know how to request transcripts when needed. This process usually involves contacting the school's registrar, filling out a transcript request form, and sometimes paying a fee. Accuracy is key, and students should review their transcripts regularly to identify and rectify any errors. When applying to colleges, it's crucial to request official transcripts well in advance of application deadlines, allowing time for processing and delivery.

Transcripts are confidential documents protected by the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), meaning they can only be released with the student's consent. With the advent of technology, many institutions now offer electronic transcripts for secure and expedited delivery. Lastly, keeping personal copies of transcripts is wise for future reference and potential needs.

What counselors need to know:

Counselors have a significant responsibility when it comes to official transcripts. They oversee the submission of these transcripts to colleges, universities, and other institutions on behalf of students, ensuring that this process is meticulously organized to meet application deadlines. Additionally, counselors must be well-versed in the regulations stipulated by the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), which safeguard the confidentiality of student records, including transcripts.

Counselors must be knowledgeable about special requirements from specific colleges or scholarship programs and provide guidance to students accordingly. They should also be proficient in handling electronic transcripts, understanding the process for sending them securely. In summary, counselors need to be well-informed, regulatory-compliant, and highly supportive as they guide students through various transcript-related matters during their educational journey. Effective communication with students and parents regarding transcript usage in contexts like college admissions or scholarship applications is fundamental to their role.
